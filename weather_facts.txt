The Earth’s tilt of approximately 23.5 degrees causes different hemispheres to receive varying intensities of sunlight throughout the year, creating the seasons.
Differential heating occurs because the equator receives more direct solar radiation than the poles, creating a temperature imbalance that the atmosphere constantly tries to level out.
The Coriolis effect, caused by the Earth's rotation, deflects moving air to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere, forming global wind patterns.
Convection currents drive weather as warm, less dense air rises and cool, denser air sinks, creating constant vertical movement in the troposphere.
Air pressure differences, or pressure gradients, cause wind to move from high-pressure areas toward low-pressure areas.
Water vapor is a primary fuel for weather; when it condenses into liquid droplets, it releases latent heat, which provides the energy for storms and clouds.
Jet streams are narrow bands of fast-moving air in the upper atmosphere that act like atmospheric rivers, steering weather systems across the globe.
Large bodies of water regulate temperature because water has a higher specific heat capacity than land, leading to milder coastal climates compared to inland areas.
Topography, such as mountain ranges, forces air to rise and cool—a process called orographic lift—which often results in heavy precipitation on one side and a rain shadow on the other.
Air masses, which are large bodies of air with uniform temperature and humidity, create "fronts" when they collide, often resulting in clouds and precipitation.
The atmosphere functions as a massive heat engine driven by the unequal distribution of solar radiation across the Earth's curved surface.
Because the equator receives more direct energy than the higher latitudes, a temperature gradient is established that the atmosphere constantly tries to level out.
Warm air at the tropics rises through convection, creating a belt of low pressure known as the Intertropical Convergence Zone where thunderstorms are frequent.
As this air rises, it undergoes adiabatic cooling, meaning it loses temperature as it expands into lower pressure without losing its actual heat energy.
When this rising air reaches the top of the troposphere, it spreads toward the poles but is deflected by the Coriolis effect, a result of the Earth's rotation.
This deflection causes the air to sink around 30 degrees latitude, creating high-pressure belts where the world’s largest deserts are located.
The sinking air in these high-pressure zones warms and dries out, which inhibits the formation of clouds and precipitation.
Surface winds moving from these high-pressure areas back toward the equator are known as the Trade Winds, which were historically vital for maritime navigation.
The "fuel" for weather systems is primarily water vapor, which stores energy as latent heat during the process of evaporation.
When water vapor condenses back into liquid droplets to form clouds, it releases this latent heat, providing the buoyancy and energy that powers storms.
Narrow bands of exceptionally fast-moving air in the upper atmosphere, called jet streams, act as steering mechanisms for surface weather systems.
Ocean currents act as a global conveyor belt, moving warm water from the tropics to the poles and significantly warming the climate of coastal regions.
Specific heat capacity explains why land heats up and cools down faster than water, leading to the formation of sea breezes and land breezes.
Mountain ranges influence weather through orographic lift, where air is forced upward, cools, and drops its moisture on the windward side.
The leeward side of a mountain range often experiences a "rain shadow," resulting in a much drier climate because the air has already lost its moisture.
Air masses—large bodies of air with uniform temperature and humidity—create weather "fronts" where they collide and interact.
A cold front occurs when a dense, cold air mass wedges under a warmer air mass, often causing sudden, violent thunderstorms and a drop in temperature.
Warm fronts occur when warm air slides over a retreating cold air mass, usually resulting in steady, long-lasting precipitation and cloud cover.
Atmospheric pressure is measured by barometers, and rapidly falling pressure is a primary indicator that a storm system is approaching.
The presence of aerosols, such as dust, sea salt, or smoke, is necessary for rain to form because they serve as cloud condensation nuclei.
Without these microscopic particles, water vapor would struggle to condense into droplets, even if the air were completely saturated.
The greenhouse effect, caused by gases like carbon dioxide and methane, traps infrared radiation and prevents it from escaping into space, regulating the planet's baseline temperature.
The Earth's axial tilt of 23.5 degrees is the reason for the seasons, as it changes the angle of sunlight hitting the surface throughout the orbital year.
Thunder is the sound caused by the rapid expansion of air that has been superheated by a lightning bolt to temperatures five times hotter than the sun.
The atmosphere is a thin layer of gases that acts like a thermal blanket, held in place by Earth's gravitational pull.
The troposphere contains approximately 80% of the atmosphere's total mass and is where nearly all weather phenomena occur.
Temperature decreases with altitude in the troposphere because the air is heated from the ground up by infrared radiation, not directly by the sun.
The tropopause acts as a "ceiling" for most storms, where the cooling trend stops and temperatures begin to stabilize.
Air pressure at sea level is approximately 14.7 pounds per square inch, which is the weight of the entire column of air above that point.
High-pressure systems, or anticyclones, rotate clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and typically bring fair, clear weather.
Low-pressure systems, or cyclones, rotate counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and are associated with rising air and precipitation.
The "Standard Atmosphere" lapse rate is approximately 3.6°F for every 1,000 feet of elevation gain.
Humidity is a measure of the amount of water vapor in the air, while "relative humidity" compares that amount to the maximum the air could hold at its current temperature.
Warm air can hold significantly more water vapor than cold air, which is why tropical climates feel much more humid than polar ones.
Supercooling occurs when water droplets remain in liquid form even at temperatures well below freezing, often found in high-altitude clouds.
Glaciation is the process in a cloud where supercooled liquid water turns into ice crystals, often initiating heavy precipitation.
The Bergeron Process explains how ice crystals grow at the expense of surrounding water droplets in cold clouds, eventually falling as snow or rain.
Advection is the horizontal movement of air or moisture, such as when warm, moist air moves over a cold surface to create sea fog.
Radiation fog forms on clear, calm nights when the ground loses heat rapidly through terrestrial radiation, cooling the air immediately above it.
A temperature inversion occurs when a layer of warm air sits on top of cooler air, trapping pollutants and moisture near the ground.
The Beaufort Scale is an empirical measure that relates wind speed to observed conditions at sea or on land, ranging from "Calm" to "Hurricane."
A "Microburst" is a localized column of sinking air (downdraft) within a thunderstorm that can cause wind speeds over 100 mph.
The "Heat Index" combines air temperature and relative humidity to determine how hot the weather actually feels to the human body.
The "Wind Chill" factor calculates the rate of heat loss from exposed skin caused by the combination of low temperatures and wind speed.
Graupel, or snow pellets, forms when supercooled water droplets freeze onto a falling snowflake, creating a rounded, opaque ice particle.
Sleet occurs when snowflakes melt into raindrops while falling through a warm layer, then refreeze into ice pellets before hitting the ground.
Freezing rain happens when raindrops fall through a shallow cold layer and freeze instantly upon contact with cold surfaces like trees or power lines.
A "Gust Front" is the leading edge of cool air rushing out of a thunderstorm, often felt as a sudden cool breeze before the rain starts.
The "Dry Line" is a boundary that separates moist air from the Gulf of Mexico and dry air from the Southwestern deserts, often a focal point for severe storms.
Mammatus clouds are pouch-like structures that hang underneath the anvil of a thunderstorm, indicating extreme instability in the atmosphere.
Lenticular clouds are lens-shaped clouds that form over mountains and are often mistaken for UFOs due to their smooth, stationary appearance.
Anemometers are the primary instruments used to measure wind speed, typically using three or four cups that rotate on a vertical shaft.
Hygrometers are used to measure the moisture content in the atmosphere, often utilizing materials that expand or contract with humidity.
The "Albedo Effect" of clouds is a dual role: they reflect incoming sunlight (cooling) but also trap outgoing heat (warming).
Contrails are artificial clouds formed by the water vapor in aircraft engine exhaust freezing at high altitudes.
A "Squall Line" is a long chain of thunderstorms that can stretch for hundreds of miles, often moving ahead of a cold front.
The "Eye" of a hurricane is a region of mostly calm weather and sinking air at the center of the storm's intense rotation.
The "Eyewall" surrounding the eye contains the most intense winds and heaviest rainfall within a tropical cyclone.
Storm surges are the most deadly part of a hurricane, caused by the wind pushing a massive wall of ocean water onto the shore.
The Saffir-Simpson Scale categorizes hurricanes from 1 to 5 based solely on their sustained wind speeds.
Monsoons are seasonal shifts in wind direction caused by the temperature difference between landmasses and adjacent oceans.
The "Santa Ana" winds are hot, dry winds that blow from the interior deserts of California toward the coast, increasing wildfire risk.
A "Derecho" is a widespread, long-lived wind storm associated with a rapidly moving band of severe thunderstorms.
The "Trade Wind Inversion" is a persistent layer of warm air that limits the vertical growth of clouds in the tropics.
Isotherms are lines on a weather map that connect points of equal temperature, helping meteorologists identify air mass boundaries.
"Atmospheric Rivers" are long, narrow regions in the atmosphere that transport most of the water vapor outside of the tropics.
The "Madden-Julian Oscillation" (MJO) is a moving-weather pattern that traverses the tropics every 30 to 60 days, influencing global rain patterns.
Thunderstorms require three ingredients: moisture, an unstable atmosphere, and a "lift" mechanism like a front or mountain.
A "Supercell" is a highly organized thunderstorm with a deep, persistently rotating updraft called a mesocyclone.
The "Triple Point" on a weather map is the intersection of a cold front, a warm front, and a dry line, often a site for tornado development.
Lightning strikes the Earth approximately 100 times every second, or 8.6 million times per day.
A "Bolt from the Blue" is a lightning strike that travels horizontally away from a storm cloud before curving down to Earth, miles from the rain.
Fulgurites are glass tubes formed when lightning strikes sandy soil, instantly melting the silica into the shape of the bolt.
The "Aerosol Indirect Effect" describes how pollution can lead to more clouds with smaller droplets, making them brighter and more reflective.
Cloud seeding is a weather modification technique that involves dispersing substances like silver iodide to encourage precipitation.
The "Fujita Scale" was updated to the "Enhanced Fujita Scale" in 2007 to better reflect the correlation between wind speed and structural damage.
A "Dust Devil" is a small, rotating column of air that forms on clear, hot days when the ground heats the air unevenly.
"Snow Squalls" are brief but intense bursts of heavy snow accompanied by strong winds, drastically reducing visibility in seconds.
The "Lake Effect" occurs when cold air moves over relatively warm lake water, picking up moisture and dumping it as heavy snow downwind.
The "Urban Canopy Layer" is the air space between city buildings where wind patterns and temperatures are heavily modified by architecture.
"Global Dimming" is a reduction in the amount of direct solar radiation reaching the Earth's surface, partly due to particulate pollution.
The "Exosphere" is the outermost layer of the atmosphere, where air molecules are so sparse they can travel hundreds of miles without colliding.
A "Habub" is an intense dust storm carried on an atmospheric gravity current, often seen in arid regions like Arizona or the Sahara.
"Blue Jets" and "Red Sprites" are electrical discharges that occur high above active thunderstorms in the upper atmosphere.
"St. Elmo's Fire" is a weather phenomenon where luminous plasma is created by a corona discharge from a sharp or pointed object in a strong electric field.
The "Standard Sea Level Pressure" is 1013.25 millibars, or 29.92 inches of mercury.
"Geostrophic Wind" is a theoretical wind that results from an exact balance between the Coriolis effect and the pressure gradient force.
A "Thermocline" is a thin layer in a large body of water where temperature changes more rapidly with depth than it does in the layers above or below.
"Relative Vorticity" is a measure of the rotation of air relative to the Earth's surface, used to track the development of low-pressure centers.
A "Trough" is an elongated area of relatively low atmospheric pressure, often associated with clouds and precipitation.
A "Ridge" is an elongated area of relatively high atmospheric pressure, typically bringing sunny and dry conditions.
The "Sky Color" is blue because of Rayleigh scattering, where shorter blue wavelengths of sunlight are scattered more by atmospheric molecules.
Sunsets appear red because the light must travel through more of the atmosphere, scattering away the blue and leaving the longer red wavelengths.
A "Halo" is a ring of light that forms around the sun or moon when light is refracted through hexagonal ice crystals in cirrus clouds.
"Sundogs," or parhelia, are bright spots on either side of the sun caused by the refraction of sunlight through ice crystals.
The "Rainbow" is formed by the reflection, refraction, and dispersion of light in water droplets, always appearing opposite the sun.
A "Double Rainbow" occurs when light is reflected twice inside a raindrop, causing the colors of the second arc to be reversed.
The "Green Flash" is a rare optical phenomenon that occurs shortly after sunset or before sunrise when a green spot is visible above the sun.
"Noctilucent Clouds" are the highest clouds in Earth's atmosphere, located in the mesosphere, and are only visible during twilight.
"Polar Stratospheric Clouds" (nacreous clouds) play a key role in the chemical reactions that lead to the depletion of the ozone layer.
The "Ozone Hole" is a seasonal thinning of the ozone layer over Antarctica, primarily caused by man-made chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).
"Carbon Sequestration" is the natural or artificial process by which carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere and stored in solid or liquid form.
The "Holocene" is the current geological epoch, characterized by a relatively stable climate that allowed human civilization to flourish.
"Paleoclimatology" is the study of past climates using data from ice cores, tree rings, and sediment layers.
"Dendroclimatology" uses the width and density of tree rings to reconstruct temperature and precipitation patterns from hundreds of years ago.
The "Little Ice Age" was a period of regional cooling between the 14th and 19th centuries, particularly affecting Europe and North America.
The "Year Without a Summer" (1816) was caused by the massive volcanic eruption of Mount Tambora, which spread ash and aerosols globally.
"Volcanic Winter" occurs when volcanic ash and sulfuric acid droplets obscure the sun, causing a temporary drop in global temperatures.
"Solar Cycles" are 11-year fluctuations in the sun's activity, including changes in the levels of solar radiation and the number of sunspots.
The "Maunder Minimum" was a period of extremely low sunspot activity that coincided with the coldest part of the Little Ice Age.
"Milankovitch Cycles" describe the long-term variations in Earth's orbit and tilt that drive the timing of ice ages over tens of thousands of years.
The "Precession" of Earth's axis is a 26,000-year wobble that changes which star is the "North Star" and affects seasonal intensity.
The "Hydrological Cycle" is the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth.
"Transpiration" is the process by which plants release water vapor into the atmosphere through small pores in their leaves called stomata.
"Sublimation" is the direct transition of water from a solid (ice) to a gas (vapor) without becoming a liquid first.
The "Global Energy Budget" is the balance between the energy Earth receives from the sun and the energy it radiates back into space.
"Positive Feedback" in climate occurs when a change triggers a process that intensifies the original change, such as melting ice reducing Earth's albedo.
"Negative Feedback" is a process that buffers or reduces the impact of a change, such as increased evaporation leading to more reflective clouds.
The "Atmospheric Window" refers to certain wavelengths of infrared radiation that can pass through the atmosphere and escape into space without being absorbed.
The atmosphere is a fluid system that adheres to the laws of fluid dynamics, meaning air moves in much the same way as water in an ocean.
The "Rossby Waves" are giant meanders in high-altitude winds that significantly influence the formation of high and low-pressure systems.
A "Cut-off Low" occurs when a low-pressure system becomes displaced from the main flow of the jet stream, often leading to days of stationary rain.
Baroclinic instability is the primary mechanism for the development of mid-latitude cyclones, driven by strong temperature gradients.
The "Thermal Wind" is a theoretical wind that represents the change in the geostrophic wind with height due to horizontal temperature variations.
The "Omega Block" is a persistent atmospheric pressure pattern that resembles the Greek letter Ω, often causing long-term heatwaves or floods.
"Zonal Flow" refers to atmospheric circulation that moves primarily along lines of latitude, leading to fast-moving, consistent weather.
"Meridional Flow" occurs when air moves significantly north or south, bringing polar air to the tropics or tropical air to the poles.
The "Centrifugal Force" plays a role in curved wind flow, acting outward from the center of rotation in a high or low-pressure system.
The "Friction Layer" typically extends about 1 kilometer above the Earth's surface, where terrain and buildings disrupt wind flow.
"Vorticity" is a measure of the local rotation of air parcels; positive vorticity is associated with rising air and stormy weather.
The "Conservation of Angular Momentum" explains why air spins faster as it moves toward the center of a low-pressure system or hurricane.
"Potential Vorticity" is a quantity that combines the rotation and the vertical stretching of an air column, often used to track air masses.
The "Solenoid Effect" describes the circulation created when surfaces of constant pressure and constant density intersect at an angle.
"Isentropic Analysis" involves studying weather on surfaces of constant potential temperature rather than constant altitude or pressure.
A "Warm Core" system, like a hurricane, has its highest temperatures at the center, which lowers the central pressure and fuels the storm.
A "Cold Core" system, like most mid-latitude winter storms, has its coldest air at the center and strengthens with height.
The "Backing" of wind occurs when wind direction shifts counterclockwise with height, usually indicating cold air advection.
The "Veering" of wind occurs when wind direction shifts clockwise with height, usually indicating warm air advection.
"Standard Gravity" is slightly lower at the equator than at the poles due to the Earth's centrifugal bulge, affecting atmospheric thickness.
The "Pressure Altimeter" in an airplane is actually a barometer that translates atmospheric pressure into an estimated altitude.
"Density Altitude" is the pressure altitude corrected for non-standard temperature, which is critical for calculating aircraft performance.
"Hygroscopic" particles are substances like salt that have a natural affinity for water, making them excellent cloud condensation nuclei.
The "Collision-Coalescence" process occurs in warm clouds where larger droplets fall faster and collide with smaller ones to grow.
"Accretion" is the process of ice crystals growing by colliding with supercooled liquid water droplets.
"Aggregation" is the process of ice crystals sticking to each other to form snowflakes.
The "Snow-to-Liquid Ratio" is usually 10:1, meaning 10 inches of snow contains the same amount of water as 1 inch of rain.
A "Trough of Warm Air Aloft" (TROWAL) is a wedge of warm air pushed upward during the occlusion process of a cyclone.
"Frontogenesis" is the atmospheric process of creating a new front or strengthening an existing temperature gradient.
"Frontolysis" is the process of a front weakening and eventually disappearing as temperature differences equalize.
A "Stationary Front" occurs when the boundary between two air masses is not moving, often resulting in prolonged cloudiness.
An "Occluded Front" forms when a fast-moving cold front catches up to a warm front, lifting the warm air completely off the ground.
The "Conveyor Belt Model" of a cyclone describes the flow of warm, moist air and cold, dry air as distinct "ribbons" of movement.
The "Dry Slot" in a satellite image of a storm shows where dry air from the upper atmosphere is being pulled down into the cyclone.
"Cyclogenesis" is the term for the birth and development of a low-pressure system.
The "Gradient Wind" is a wind that accounts for both the pressure gradient force and the centrifugal force in curved flow.
"Turbulence" is caused by the irregular movement of air, often triggered by friction with the ground or intense solar heating.
"Clear Air Turbulence" (CAT) occurs at high altitudes near jet streams where there are no clouds to warn pilots of the unstable air.
A "Gravity Wave" in the atmosphere is a wave produced by the displacement of air, such as air flowing over a mountain range.
The "Froude Number" is a dimensionless parameter used to determine if air will flow over a mountain or be blocked by it.
"Anabatic Winds" are upslope winds driven by the heating of a mountain face during the day.
"Mountain-Valley Breezes" are diurnal wind patterns caused by the differential heating of sloped terrain versus the valley floor.
The "Heat Island" of a city can actually create its own localized low-pressure system, drawing in cooler air from the suburbs.
"Roughness Length" is a parameter in meteorology that describes how much the surface (trees, buildings, grass) slows down the wind.
The "Ekman Spiral" describes how wind direction changes and speed decreases as you move from the free atmosphere down to the surface.
"Teleconnections" are climate anomalies that relate weather changes in one part of the world to changes in another, thousands of miles away.
The "Arctic Oscillation" (AO) is a back-and-forth shifting of atmospheric pressure between the Arctic and the mid-latitudes.
The "North Atlantic Oscillation" (NAO) influences the strength and direction of the jet stream over the Atlantic and Europe.
"Pacific Decadal Oscillation" (PDO) is a long-term ocean-atmosphere climate variability that can last 20 to 30 years.
The "Southern Oscillation Index" (SOI) measures the pressure difference between Tahiti and Darwin, Australia, to track El Niño.
A "La Niña" event is characterized by unusually cold ocean temperatures in the Equatorial Pacific, often the opposite of El Niño.
"Upwelling" brings cold, nutrient-rich water to the surface, which can cool the local atmosphere and increase fish populations.
The "Thermocline" in the ocean is pushed deeper during El Niño, preventing the upwelling of cold water.
"Greenhouse Gas Residence Time" varies greatly; water vapor stays for days, while CO2​ can remain for centuries.
"Radiative Equilibrium" occurs when the energy absorbed by the Earth equals the energy emitted back into space.
"Optical Depth" is a measure of how much light is prevented from passing through a column of the atmosphere by clouds or dust.
The "Solar Constant" actually fluctuates by about 0.1% over the course of the 11-year solar cycle.
"Shortwave Radiation" refers to the high-energy light from the sun, including visible, UV, and near-infrared.
"Longwave Radiation" refers to the lower-energy infrared heat emitted by the Earth's surface and atmosphere.
The "Atmospheric Window" is the range of infrared wavelengths that CO2​ and water vapor do not absorb well.
"Global Warming Potential" (GWP) is a measure of how much energy the emissions of 1 ton of a gas will absorb over a given period.
The "Lapse Rate" in the stratosphere is negative, meaning it gets warmer as you go higher.
The "Mesopause" is the boundary between the mesosphere and the thermosphere and is the coldest point in the entire atmosphere.
"Plasma" in the thermosphere consists of gas atoms that have been stripped of their electrons by intense solar radiation.
"Sky Glow" is the brightening of the night sky caused by man-made light reflecting off particles in the atmosphere.
The "Aureole" is the bright, fuzzy circle of light often seen immediately surrounding the sun or moon through a thin haze.
"Crepuscular Rays" are sunbeams that seem to radiate from a single point in the sky, often seen at sunset or sunrise.
"Anti-crepuscular Rays" are similar to crepuscular rays but appear on the opposite side of the sky from the sun.
"Scintillation" is the scientific term for the twinkling of stars caused by turbulence in the Earth's atmosphere.
"Refraction" causes the sun to appear slightly higher in the sky than it actually is, especially when it is near the horizon.
The "Novaya Zemlya Effect" is a polar mirage where the sun appears to rise earlier or set later than it should.
A "Fata Morgana" is a complex, superior mirage that can make distant objects look like floating castles or mountains.
"Looming" is a mirage where objects below the horizon are refracted so they appear to be floating in the air.
The "Incus" or anvil cloud is the flat top of a cumulonimbus cloud, shaped by the high winds at the tropopause.
"Pileus" clouds are small, cap-like clouds that form briefly above a rapidly growing cumulus tower.
"Virga" often appears as grey streaks hanging from a cloud, indicating rain that is evaporating in dry air below.
"Fallstreaks" are holes in cirrocumulus or altocumulus clouds caused by falling ice crystals.
"Cirrus" clouds are composed entirely of ice crystals and usually indicate a change in weather is 24 to 48 hours away.
"Stratus" clouds are uniform, grey clouds that cover the entire sky like a blanket, often producing drizzle.
"Cumulus" clouds are puffy, white clouds with flat bases that form due to localized pockets of rising warm air.
"Altostratus" clouds are mid-level grey sheets that are thin enough to let the sun show through as if through frosted glass.
"Nimbostratus" clouds are dark, thick stratus clouds that produce steady, continuous rain or snow.
The "Cloud Base" height can be estimated by multiplying the difference between the temperature and dew point by 400 (in feet).
"Saturation" occurs when the rate of evaporation equals the rate of condensation.
"Dynamic Pressure" is the pressure exerted by a moving fluid, such as the wind pushing against a building.
"Static Pressure" is the pressure of the air when it is not in motion.
The "Bernoulli Principle" states that as the speed of a moving fluid increases, its pressure decreases.
"Vortex Shedding" is the process of wind creating oscillating eddies behind an object, which can cause structures to vibrate.
A "Dust Storm" can reduce visibility to zero and can be seen from space as a massive plume of brown or tan.
"Sea Salt Aerosols" are produced by breaking waves and are one of the most common types of condensation nuclei over the ocean.
"Biogenic Aerosols" include pollen and spores, which can also influence cloud formation and precipitation.
"Global Stilling" is a phenomenon observed in some regions where average surface wind speeds have decreased over recent decades.
The "Doldrums" is the nautical term for the ITCZ, where sailing ships could be stranded for weeks due to lack of wind.
The "Scale Height" of the atmosphere is approximately 8.5 kilometers, which is the vertical distance over which the pressure drops by a factor of e.
"Static Stability" refers to the atmosphere's resistance to vertical motion; a stable atmosphere suppresses upward air movement.
An "Adiabatic Process" is one where no heat is exchanged between an air parcel and its surroundings, only work through expansion or compression.
The "Dry Adiabatic Lapse Rate" is approximately 9.8°C per kilometer, the rate at which an unsaturated air parcel cools as it rises.
The "Saturated Adiabatic Lapse Rate" is lower (around 5°C to 6°C per kilometer) because the release of latent heat during condensation offsets some of the cooling.
"Potential Temperature" (θ) is the temperature an air parcel would have if it were brought dry-adiabatically to a standard pressure of 1000 hPa.
The "Brunt-Väisälä Frequency" is the frequency at which an air parcel will oscillate vertically when displaced in a stable atmosphere.
"Boundary Layer Turbulence" is strongest during the afternoon when solar heating of the ground is at its peak.
The "Roughness Sublayer" is the very bottom of the atmosphere where individual obstacles like trees and buildings create unique micro-eddies.
"Inertial Oscillations" occur when the wind is disturbed and the Coriolis force causes it to rotate in a circle over a period of many hours.
The "Ageostrophic Wind" is the difference between the actual wind and the theoretical geostrophic wind, responsible for vertical motions in the atmosphere.
"Convergence" occurs when more air enters a region than leaves it, forcing the excess air to move upward or downward.
"Divergence" in the upper atmosphere acts like a vacuum, pulling air up from the surface to create low-pressure systems.
The "Level of Non-Divergence" (LND) is an altitude, usually around 500 hPa, where the horizontal divergence is zero.
"Cyclonic Shear" occurs when wind speed changes across a horizontal distance in a way that encourages counterclockwise rotation.
"Anticyclonic Shear" occurs when wind speed changes encourage clockwise rotation, often associated with high-pressure ridges.
The "Rossby Number" is a dimensionless value used to describe whether the Coriolis force or inertial forces dominate a particular flow.
A low Rossby Number (much less than 1) indicates that the Earth's rotation significantly influences the wind pattern.
The "Hadley Cell" is the primary circulation loop where air rises at the equator and sinks at the subtropics.
The "Ferrel Cell" is the mid-latitude circulation loop where air moves poleward and eastward near the surface.
The "Polar Cell" is the smallest circulation loop, where cold air sinks at the poles and moves toward the mid-latitudes.
The "Polar Front" is the boundary where cold polar air meets warmer mid-latitude air, serving as a breeding ground for storms.
The "Subtropical Jet Stream" is located at higher altitudes than the Polar Jet and is driven by the conservation of angular momentum from the Hadley Cell.
"Geopotential Height" is a vertical coordinate adjusted for the slight variations in Earth's gravity at different altitudes and latitudes.
"Thickness" on a weather map is the vertical distance between two pressure levels, which is directly proportional to the average temperature of that air layer.
A "Shortwave Trough" is a small-scale ripple in the upper-level flow that can trigger the development of surface thunderstorms.
"Positive Vorticity Advection" (PVA) occurs when the wind moves higher values of rotation into a region, often leading to rising air.
"Cold Air Damming" occurs when a high-pressure system forces cold air against a mountain range, where it gets trapped and creates a shallow cold layer.
The "Low-Level Jet" (LLJ) is a fast-moving stream of air in the lower atmosphere, often responsible for transporting moisture into the Great Plains.
"Nocturnal Inversions" form as the ground cools at night, creating a stable layer that can trap fog or smoke near the surface.
"Isallobars" are lines on a map connecting points that have experienced the same change in atmospheric pressure over a set time.
The "Pressure Tendency" is the character and amount of atmospheric pressure change over a three-hour period.
"Streamlines" are lines that are tangent to the wind vector at every point, providing a snapshot of the flow field.
"Back-building" thunderstorms are storms where new cells continually form on the upwind side, often leading to localized flooding.
A "Training" storm occurs when multiple cells move over the same area in quick succession, like railroad cars on a track.
The "Bow Echo" is a characteristic radar signature of a line of thunderstorms that has been "pushed" out by strong internal winds.
A "Rear-Inflow Jet" is a flow of air that develops behind a squall line, accelerating toward the front and causing damaging surface winds.
"Total Totals Index" (TT) is a stability index used to assess the potential for severe thunderstorm development.
The "K-Index" is a measure of thunderstorm potential based on the vertical temperature lapse rate and the moisture content of the lower atmosphere.
"Precipitable Water" (PW) is the total amount of water vapor in a vertical column of air if it were all condensed into liquid.
"Entrainment" of dry air into a thunderstorm can cause "Evaporative Cooling," which strengthens the storm's downdraft.
"Wet Microbursts" occur with heavy precipitation, while "Dry Microbursts" occur when rain evaporates before hitting the ground.
"Heat Bursts" are rare phenomena where a dying thunderstorm produces a sudden localized spike in temperature and a drop in humidity.
The "Outflow Boundary" or "Mesohigh" is a small-scale high-pressure system created by the cold downdraft of a thunderstorm.
"Graupel" serves as an important factor in cloud electrification; collisions between graupel and ice crystals generate static charges.
"Stepped Leaders" are the initial, invisible channels of ionized air that "step" down from a cloud toward the ground before a lightning strike.
The "Return Stroke" is the bright, visible part of a lightning bolt that travels from the ground back up to the cloud.
"Cloud-to-Air" lightning occurs when a discharge moves from a cloud into the surrounding positively or negatively charged air.
"Spider Lightning" refers to long, horizontal flashes that crawl across the underside of stratiform clouds.
"Thunder" cannot be heard more than about 10 to 15 miles away because the sound waves are refracted upward into the atmosphere.
A "Storm Cell" typically has a life cycle of about 30 to 60 minutes, consisting of the cumulus, mature, and dissipating stages.
"Multi-cell" storms consist of a cluster of cells at different stages of their life cycles, allowing the system to last for several hours.
"Helicity" is a measure of the intensity of rotation in the lower atmosphere, used to predict the likelihood of tornadoes.
The "Wall Cloud" is a localized lowering of the cloud base in a supercell, where the most intense rotation is often located.
"Suction Vortices" are smaller, intense sub-circulations that can rotate around the center of a large tornado.
The "Tornado Alley" region in the U.S. exists because it is the meeting point of cold dry air from Canada and warm moist air from the Gulf.
"Waterspouts" are tornadoes that form over water; "Fair Weather Waterspouts" develop from the surface up rather than from a rotating storm.
"Barometric Pressure" at the center of a tornado can be as much as 100 millibars lower than the surrounding environment.
"Tropical Cyclones" require sea surface temperatures of at least 26.5°C (80°F) to form and maintain their intensity.
The "Beta Effect" describes how the Earth's change in Coriolis force with latitude causes hurricanes to naturally drift toward the northwest.
"Vertical Wind Shear" is the enemy of hurricanes; it tilts the storm's core and prevents the efficient release of latent heat.
The "Radius of Maximum Winds" is the distance from the center of a hurricane to where the strongest winds are located.
"Rapid Intensification" (RI) is defined as an increase in a tropical cyclone's maximum sustained winds of at least 35 knots in 24 hours.
"Post-Tropical Transition" occurs when a hurricane moves into cooler waters and begins to take on the characteristics of a mid-latitude storm.
"Swell" refers to wind-generated waves that have traveled away from their area of origin, often reaching distant shores before a storm arrives.
"Rip Currents" are powerful, narrow channels of fast-moving water that are often stronger during or after major offshore storms.
"Paleotempestology" is the study of past tropical cyclone activity using geological evidence like overwash sand layers in coastal lakes.
The "Maunder Minimum" (1645–1715) saw almost no sunspots and coincided with some of the coldest winters on record.
"Solar Wind" consists of a stream of energized, charged particles, primarily electrons and protons, flowing outward from the Sun.
"Geomagnetic Storms" caused by solar flares can compress the Earth's magnetosphere and affect long-range radio weather transmissions.
"Isopycnals" are lines of constant density in the ocean, which play a major role in the vertical movement of water.
"Haloclines" are vertical zones in the ocean where salinity changes rapidly with depth, affecting water density and local weather.
The "North Pacific Garbage Patch" is held in place by the North Pacific Gyre, a large system of rotating ocean currents.
"El Niño-Southern Oscillation" (ENSO) neutral conditions occur when sea surface temperatures are near the long-term average.
The "Walker Circulation" is the longitudinal (east-west) circulation of air across the Pacific Ocean.
"Atmospheric Pressure" on Venus is 92 times greater than on Earth, creating weather dominated by a massive greenhouse effect.
"Mars" has global dust storms that can last for weeks and obscure the entire surface of the planet from view.
"Jupiter's Great Red Spot" is a high-pressure anticyclonic storm that has been raging for at least 350 years.
"Diamond Rain" is theorized to occur in the high-pressure atmospheres of Uranus and Neptune.
"Titan," Saturn's moon, has a methane cycle similar to Earth's water cycle, featuring methane clouds and rain.
The "Dry Line" is often visible on satellite imagery as a sharp boundary where cumulus clouds form on the moist side.
"Synoptic Scale" meteorology studies weather systems that cover areas of several hundred to several thousand square kilometers.
"Mesoscale" meteorology focuses on smaller phenomena like thunderstorms, sea breezes, and mountain waves.
"Microscale" meteorology looks at the smallest movements of air, such as the turbulence around a single tree or building.
The "Numerical Weather Prediction" (NWP) uses mathematical models of the atmosphere to forecast the weather based on current conditions.
"Ensemble Forecasting" involves running a weather model multiple times with slightly different starting conditions to see the range of possible outcomes.
"Chaos Theory" explains why weather is difficult to predict long-term; small errors in the initial data grow exponentially over time.
The "Butterfly Effect" is a metaphorical illustration of how a small change in one part of a system can lead to large differences in a later state.
"Radiosondes" are instruments carried by weather balloons that measure pressure, temperature, and humidity as they rise.
"Lidar" (Light Detection and Ranging) is used to measure wind speed and atmospheric composition by bouncing laser light off particles.
"Doppler Radar" measures the change in frequency of returned energy to determine the velocity of raindrops toward or away from the radar.
"Dual-Polarization Radar" sends both horizontal and vertical pulses, allowing meteorologists to identify the shape and type of precipitation.
"GOES" (Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellites) orbit at the same speed as the Earth's rotation, allowing them to stay over the same spot.
"POES" (Polar Operational Environmental Satellites) orbit the Earth from pole to pole, providing high-resolution data for the entire globe twice a day.
The "Barotropic" atmosphere is a theoretical state where surfaces of constant pressure and constant density are parallel, meaning no thermal wind exists.
A "Baroclinic" atmosphere occurs when temperature gradients exist on a constant pressure surface, providing the potential energy for major storms.
"Vorticity Advection" is the horizontal transport of rotation by the wind, a key predictor for where surface low-pressure systems will intensify.
The "Rossby Radius of Deformation" is the length scale at which rotational effects (Coriolis) become as important as buoyancy effects in a fluid.
"Isopycnic" surfaces in the ocean are layers of constant water density, which act as "highways" for the movement of deep-sea nutrients and heat.
The "Mixed Layer" of the ocean is the top layer where wind-driven turbulence keeps the temperature and salinity uniform.
"Thermobaric" effects occur in deep water when the compressibility of seawater changes its density enough to trigger vertical sinking.
"Inertial Stability" is a measure of the atmosphere's resistance to horizontal displacements, separate from vertical buoyancy.
"Cyclostrophic Balance" occurs in small, intense vortices like tornadoes where the pressure gradient force is balanced by centrifugal force rather than Coriolis.
The "Boussinesq Approximation" is a mathematical simplification used in weather models that ignores density variations except where they contribute to buoyancy.
"Reynolds Stress" represents the transfer of momentum due to turbulent fluctuations in the wind, critical for modeling the boundary layer.
"Taylor-Görtler Vortices" are counter-rotating streaks of air that form when flow over a curved surface becomes unstable.
"Potential Temperature Lapse Rate" determines the static stability; if potential temperature increases with height, the atmosphere is stable.
"Equivalent Potential Temperature" (θe​) accounts for the latent heat released if all water vapor in an air parcel were to condense.
"Absolute Vorticity" is the sum of the Earth's rotation (planetary vorticity) and the air's rotation relative to the ground (relative vorticity).
"Divergence aloft" acts like a "vent" for a surface low-pressure system, allowing more air to rise and the central pressure to drop.
A "Confluence Zone" is a region where streamlines move closer together, which can lead to the strengthening of temperature gradients.
A "Diffluence Zone" is where streamlines spread apart, often found downstream of a trough in the jet stream.
"Geostrophic Adjustment" is the process by which the mass field and the wind field reach a state of balance after a disturbance.
The "Eady Model" is a classic mathematical framework used to explain how mid-latitude cyclones grow from small perturbations in a temperature gradient.
"Symmetric Instability" can lead to the formation of slanted narrow bands of heavy precipitation, often seen in winter storms.
"Frontal Uplift" provides the mechanical "shove" needed to move air parcels to their Level of Free Convection.
"Conditional Instability" describes an atmosphere that is stable for dry air but unstable if the air becomes saturated and releases latent heat.
"Absolute Instability" occurs when the environmental lapse rate is greater than the dry adiabatic lapse rate, leading to spontaneous convection.
"Isentropic Potential Vorticity" (IPV) maps are used by meteorologists to identify "stratospheric intrusions" where dry, ozone-rich air descends.
A "Tropopause Fold" is a structural deformation where stratospheric air is pulled down into the troposphere near a strong jet stream.
"Jet Entrance Regions" are areas where air accelerates into a jet streak, creating vertical circulations that can trigger storm development.
"Jet Exit Regions" are where air decelerates, often leading to divergence in the left-exit quadrant which supports surface low pressure.
The "Petterssen Development Equation" relates surface pressure changes to the movement of temperature and vorticity patterns aloft.
"Q-Vectors" are mathematical tools used to diagnose vertical motion without having to calculate divergence directly from wind data.
The "Omega Equation" is the fundamental diagnostic equation used to determine where air is rising or sinking in the large-scale atmosphere.
"Stretched Vorticity" occurs when a rotating column of air is pulled vertically, causing it to spin faster (the figure skater effect).
"Tilted Vorticity" happens when horizontal rotation (caused by wind shear) is turned into vertical rotation by a thunderstorm updraft.
"Helicity" is calculated as the area on a hodograph, representing the potential for a storm to acquire a rotating updraft.
A "Mesocyclone" is a 2 to 10-kilometer wide vortex of air rotating within a convective storm.
"BWER" (Bounded Weak Echo Region) is a radar signature showing an intense updraft so strong that precipitation cannot fall through it.
A "Hook Echo" on radar is caused by precipitation being wrapped around the rotating mesocyclone of a supercell.
"TVS" (Tornado Vortex Signature) is a specific radar detection of intense, localized gate-to-gate wind shear.
The "V-Notch" on a satellite or radar image indicates air flowing around a very strong thunderstorm updraft.
"Anvil Zits" are small, short-lived towers that pop up on the top of a thunderstorm's anvil.
"Aspirated Psychrometers" use a fan to pull air over a wet-bulb thermometer to ensure an accurate humidity reading.
"Radiosonde Drift" must be accounted for in models, as a weather balloon can travel over 100 miles from its launch site.
"Dropsondes" are instruments dropped from aircraft into hurricanes to measure the storm's internal structure as they fall to the sea.
"ASOS" (Automated Surface Observing Systems) provide the continuous stream of data used for airport weather reports (METARs).
"Ceilometers" use laser pulses to measure the exact height of the cloud base above an airfield.
"Disstromeeters" are used to measure the size distribution and velocity of individual falling raindrops.
"Snow Pillows" are large, fluid-filled bladders used in mountains to weigh the snowpack and calculate its water equivalent.
"Pyranometers" measure the total solar irradiance reaching a flat surface from the entire hemisphere of the sky.
"Pyrgeometers" specifically measure the longwave (infrared) radiation coming down from the atmosphere or up from the ground.
"Eddy Covariance" towers measure the high-speed exchange of CO2​ and water vapor between the ground and the air.
"Numerical Diffusion" is a type of error in weather models where sharp boundaries (like fronts) become "smeared" over time.
"Grid Resolution" determines the smallest feature a model can see; a 3km grid can "resolve" individual storms, while a 100km grid cannot.
"Parameterization" is the method of using simplified formulas to represent processes too small for the model grid, like cloud droplets or friction.
"Data Assimilation" is the complex process of combining previous model forecasts with new observations to create a starting "analysis."
The "Global Forecast System" (GFS) is the primary American long-range weather model.
The "European Model" (ECMWF) is often considered the most accurate global model due to its advanced data assimilation system.
"HRRR" (High-Resolution Rapid Refresh) is a short-range model that updates every hour to track active severe weather.
"Teleconnection Patterns" like the PNA (Pacific-North American) pattern describe how pressure over the Pacific dictates weather in the US.
The "Arctic Sea Ice Albedo Feedback" is a process where melting ice exposes dark ocean, which absorbs more heat and melts more ice.
"Permafrost Thaw" releases ancient methane and carbon dioxide, acting as a potent positive feedback for global warming.
"Ocean Acidification" occurs as the sea absorbs excess CO2​ from the atmosphere, turning it into carbonic acid.
"The Thermohaline Circulation" can be slowed down if too much fresh meltwater from glaciers enters the North Atlantic.
"Carbon Sinks" like forests and oceans currently absorb about half of the CO2​ humans emit into the atmosphere.
"Aerosol-Cloud Interactions" are one of the biggest uncertainties in predicting future climate sensitivity.
"Solar Radiation Management" (SRM) is a theoretical geoengineering idea to reflect sunlight by injecting aerosols into the stratosphere.
"Carbon Capture and Storage" (CCS) involves stripping CO2​ from industrial exhaust and pumping it underground.
"Urban Heat Island Intensity" is usually highest at night when buildings slowly release the heat they stored during the day.
"Biometeorology" studies how weather affects living organisms, including human health and crop yields.
"Phenology" is the study of the timing of biological events, like the first frost or the blooming of flowers, in relation to climate.
"Growing Degree Days" (GDD) is a measure used by farmers to predict when a crop will reach maturity based on daily temperatures.
"Cold Stress" in cattle can be predicted using the "Comprehensive Climate Index," which combines wind, temp, and humidity.
"Aviation Weather" focuses heavily on icing, which occurs when supercooled droplets freeze onto the wings of a plane.
"Carburetor Icing" can happen in aircraft even on warm days if the humidity is high and the pressure drop in the venturi cools the air.
"Turbulent Kinetic Energy" (TKE) is a measure of the intensity of "churn" in the atmosphere, used to forecast aviation safety.
"Mountain Waves" can create "Rotors," which are violent, horizontal-axis vortices that are extremely dangerous to small aircraft.
"Downsloping" winds like the Chinook can cause temperatures to rise by 40°F in just a few minutes through adiabatic compression.
"Foehn Winds" are the European equivalent of Chinooks, often bringing dry, warm air to the valleys of the Alps.
"Mistral" is a cold, dry wind that blows from the north down into the Mediterranean through the Rhone Valley.
"Bora" is a cold, katabatic wind that affects the Adriatic Sea, sometimes reaching hurricane speeds.
"Sirocco" is a Mediterranean wind that carries hot, dusty air from the Sahara Desert toward Southern Europe.
"Harmattan" is a dry, dusty West African trade wind that blows from the Sahara toward the Gulf of Guinea.
"Leste" is a hot, dry wind that blows from the African continent toward the Canary Islands.
"Willy-Willy" is an Australian term for a dust devil or a small whirlwind.
"Soul-searching winds" is a folk term for the persistent, dry winds that can affect human mood and behavior in certain regions.
"Thunderstorm Asthma" is a phenomenon where storm downdrafts concentrate pollen and spores at ground level, triggering mass allergic reactions.
"Red Tides" (harmful algal blooms) can be influenced by wind-driven upwelling and rainfall runoff carrying nutrients into the sea.
"Sandstorms" (Haboobs) can transport fungi and bacteria thousands of miles across oceans.
"Ice Storms" occur when a "Warm Nose" of air exists aloft, allowing snow to melt and then freeze on cold surfaces below.
"Thundersnow" is a rare event where lightning occurs during a snowstorm, usually requiring intense upward motion in a cold environment.
"Diamond Dust" consists of tiny ice crystals that fall from a clear sky in extremely cold polar regions.
"Sun Pillars" are vertical shafts of light extending above or below the sun, caused by light reflecting off flat ice crystals.
"Glory" is an optical phenomenon appearing as concentric colored rings around the observer's shadow on a cloud or fog.
"The Brockenspectre" is a large shadow of an observer cast onto a cloud, often surrounded by a glory, seen from mountains.
"Heiligenschein" is a bright spot around the shadow of an observer's head, caused by dew drops on grass acting as lenses.
"Crepuscular Rays" are also known as "Jacob’s Ladder" or "God’s rays" in various cultures.
"Anti-Solar Point" is the imaginary spot in the sky exactly opposite the sun, where rainbows and glories are centered.
"Refractive Index" of air changes with temperature and pressure, causing the "shimmering" seen over a hot road.
A "Trough" in the upper atmosphere is an area of relatively low pressure that often serves as the catalyst for surface-level storm development.
A "Ridge" is an area of high pressure that typically results in sinking air, suppressed cloud formation, and warmer-than-average temperatures.
"Thermal Advection" is the process by which wind moves air of a different temperature into a new region.
"Cold Air Advection" occurs when wind blows from a colder region toward a warmer one, often seen behind a passing cold front.
"Warm Air Advection" occurs when wind blows from a warmer region toward a colder one, often leading to rising motion and light precipitation.
"Moisture Advection" is the horizontal transport of water vapor, essential for fueling large thunderstorm complexes.
The "Mixing Ratio" remains constant unless water vapor is added to or removed from an air parcel, making it a reliable measure for tracking air masses.
"Vapor Pressure Deficit" (VPD) is the difference between the amount of moisture the air can hold and how much it currently holds, a key metric for plant transpiration.
"Standard Sea Level Pressure" is defined as 1013.25 hectopascals (hPa) or 29.92 inches of mercury (inHg).
"Barometric Tendency" refers to whether the air pressure is rising, falling, or steady, which helps sailors and meteorologists predict short-term weather changes.
The "Hypsometric Equation" relates the thickness of an atmospheric layer between two pressure surfaces to the layer's mean temperature.
"Geopotential" is the work required to lift a unit mass from sea level to a specific height, accounting for variations in gravity.
"Constant Pressure Maps" (like the 500mb chart) are used instead of constant height maps because they simplify the equations of atmospheric motion.
"Isopleths" is the general term for any line on a map connecting points of equal value, such as isobars (pressure) or isotherms (temperature).
The "Coriolis Force" is zero at the equator and reaches its maximum strength at the poles.
"Geostrophic Wind" occurs when the pressure gradient force and the Coriolis force are in perfect balance, resulting in wind parallel to isobars.
"Gradient Wind" accounts for the extra centrifugal force felt when air moves in a curved path around high or low-pressure centers.
"Surface Friction" causes wind to cross isobars at an angle toward lower pressure, which is why air spirals into a cyclone's center.
The "Ekman Layer" is the transition zone where the effect of surface friction gradually disappears with increasing altitude.
"Vertical Velocity" (w) in the atmosphere is usually measured in centimeters per second, much slower than horizontal wind speeds.
"Convergence" at the surface forces air to rise, while "Divergence" at the surface causes air to sink.
"Dines’ Compensation Principle" states that convergence in one layer of the atmosphere must be balanced by divergence in another layer.
"Vorticity" is the measure of "spin" in the atmosphere; a spinning ice skater illustrates the conservation of vorticity.
"Planetary Vorticity" is the spin inherited from the Earth's rotation, which increases as you move from the equator toward the poles.
"Relative Vorticity" is the spin of the air relative to the Earth's surface, caused by wind shear or curved flow.
"Absolute Vorticity" is the sum of planetary and relative vorticity.
"Potential Vorticity" (PV) is often used to identify the "Dynamic Tropopause," where stratospheric air meets tropospheric air.
"Rossby Waves" are the long-wave meanders in the jet stream that move weather systems from west to east.
"Zonal Index" is a measure of the strength of the west-to-east flow; a high index means fast, straight winds, while a low index means wavy, erratic winds.
"Blocking Anticyclones" are massive high-pressure systems that stay stationary for weeks, "blocking" the normal path of storms.
The "Rex Block" consists of a high-pressure system located directly north of a low-pressure system, causing stagnant weather in between.
"Teleconnections" like the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) explain why a warm ocean in the Pacific can cause a rainy winter in Florida.
The "North Atlantic Oscillation" (NAO) influences the track of winter storms across the Atlantic toward Europe or New England.
"Arctic Oscillation" (AO) describes the state of the wind circling the Arctic; a "negative phase" often sends freezing polar air deep into the mid-latitudes.
"Pacific-North American" (PNA) pattern is a teleconnection that dictates whether the western US is in a ridge or a trough.
"Madden-Julian Oscillation" (MJO) is a "pulse" of clouds and rain that moves eastward around the equator every 30 to 60 days.
"Hydrostatic Equilibrium" is the balance between the downward force of gravity and the upward force of air pressure.
"Archimedes' Principle" applies to the atmosphere: an air parcel will rise if it is less dense (warmer) than the air surrounding it.
"Convection" is the primary way the atmosphere moves heat vertically from the surface into the upper air.
"Free Convection" occurs when air rises solely due to buoyancy (like a hot air balloon).
"Forced Convection" occurs when air is pushed up by a front, a mountain, or surface convergence.
The "Level of Free Convection" (LFC) is the height at which an air parcel becomes warmer than its environment and begins rising on its own.
"Convective Available Potential Energy" (CAPE) is the "fuel" for thunderstorms; higher values mean more explosive upward growth.
"Convective Inhibition" (CIN) is the "cap" that prevents storms from forming, often a layer of warm air just above the surface.
"Entrainment" is the mixing of dry environmental air into a growing cloud, which can cause the cloud to evaporate and stop growing.
"Detrainment" is the opposite of entrainment, where a cloud pushes its moist air out into the surrounding environment.
"Cloud Condensation Nuclei" (CCN) are tiny particles like sea salt or smoke that provide a surface for water vapor to condense upon.
"Ice Nuclei" are much rarer than CCN and are required for ice crystals to form at temperatures between 0°C and -38°C.
"Homogeneous Nucleation" is the freezing of pure water droplets without any nuclei, which only happens at temperatures below -38°C (-36°F).
The "Bergeron-Findeisen Process" explains why ice crystals grow at the expense of liquid droplets in cold clouds, leading to precipitation.
"Collision-Coalescence" is the process by which rain forms in "warm" clouds (above freezing) as larger drops bump into and merge with smaller ones.
"Accretion" (or riming) is the process of ice crystals colliding with supercooled water droplets, forming graupel or hail.
"Aggregation" is the clumping together of ice crystals to form snowflakes.
"Terminal Velocity" is the constant speed a raindrop reaches when the upward air resistance equals the downward pull of gravity.
Large raindrops fall faster than small ones, but they also break apart if they grow larger than about 6 millimeters in diameter.
"Virga" is rain that evaporates before it hits the ground, often seen as "curtains" hanging from the base of high clouds.
"Aerosols" from volcanic eruptions can stay in the stratosphere for years, reflecting sunlight and cooling the planet.
"Scattering" is the process where gas molecules or particles redirect light in all directions.
"Rayleigh Scattering" is responsible for the blue sky because smaller molecules scatter shorter (blue) wavelengths more efficiently.
"Mie Scattering" occurs when particles are similar in size to the wavelength of light, making clouds appear white.
"Non-selective Scattering" occurs with large water drops, which scatter all wavelengths equally, making thick clouds appear grey.
The "Albedo" of the Earth is approximately 30%, meaning nearly a third of incoming sunlight is reflected back into space.
"Greenhouse Gases" absorb longwave infrared radiation emitted by the Earth, trapping heat in the atmosphere.
"Water Vapor" is the most significant greenhouse gas by volume, but "Carbon Dioxide" is the most significant driver of human-induced climate change.
"Radiative Forcing" is the measurement of the capacity of a gas or other forcing agent to affect that energy balance.
"Latent Heat" is the energy released or absorbed during a phase change (like water freezing or evaporating) without a change in temperature.
The "Standard Lapse Rate" is a temperature decrease of about 6.5°C for every 1000 meters of altitude.
An "Inversion" is a layer where temperature increases with height, acting as a lid that traps smoke, smog, and moisture.
"Radiation Inversions" form on clear, calm nights as the ground cools rapidly, chilling the air immediately above it.
"Subsidence Inversions" form when air sinks over a large area, warming adiabatically and creating a warm layer above cooler surface air.
"Frontal Inversions" occur when warm air is pushed over a wedge of cold air at a weather front.
"Marine Inversions" are common along coasts where cool, moist air from the ocean slides under warmer air over the land.
"Advection Fog" forms when warm, moist air moves over a cold surface, like the famous fog in San Francisco.
"Steam Fog" (or "Sea Smoke") forms when very cold air moves over relatively warm water, making the water appear to "smoke."
"Upslope Fog" forms as air is pushed up a mountain and cools to its dew point.
"Dew Point" is the temperature to which air must be cooled to become saturated with water vapor.
"Frost Point" is the equivalent of dew point when the temperature is below freezing.
"Relative Humidity" tells you how close the air is to being saturated, but it doesn't tell you the actual amount of water in the air.
"Heat Index" calculates the "apparent temperature" by combining air temperature and relative humidity.
"Wind Chill" is a measure of the rate of heat loss from exposed skin caused by wind and cold temperatures.
"Laminar Flow" is smooth, non-turbulent air movement, while "Turbulent Flow" is characterized by chaotic eddies.
"Mechanical Turbulence" is caused by wind blowing over obstacles like trees, mountains, or buildings.
"Thermal Turbulence" (Convective Turbulence) is caused by rising thermals of warm air on a sunny day.
"Clear Air Turbulence" (CAT) is high-altitude turbulence that occurs without clouds, often near the edges of a jet stream.
"Mountain Waves" (Lee Waves) are ripples in the air caused by wind blowing over a mountain range, sometimes visible as lenticular clouds.
"Rotors" are violent, horizontal-axis vortices that form under the crests of mountain waves and are extremely dangerous to aircraft.
A "Microburst" is a powerful, localized downdraft that spreads out in all directions when it hits the ground.
"St. Elmo's Fire" is a glowing blue or violet light seen on pointed objects during thunderstorms, caused by an electrical discharge.
"Ball Lightning" is a rare and poorly understood phenomenon where a glowing sphere of electricity appears during a storm.
"Red Sprites" and "Blue Jets" are types of "Transient Luminous Events" that occur in the upper atmosphere above thunderstorms.
"Noctilucent Clouds" are the highest clouds in the atmosphere (80km up) and can only be seen during twilight in the summer.
"Nacreous Clouds" (Mother-of-Pearl clouds) form in the stratosphere and are known for their brilliant, iridescent colors.
"Synoptic Meteorology" deals with the study of weather systems on a scale of 1,000 kilometers or more, like major cyclones.
"Mesometeorology" focuses on medium-scale events (1km to 1,000km) like thunderstorms, sea breezes, and squall lines.
"Micrometeorology" is the study of atmospheric processes in the lowest few hundred meters, focusing on turbulence and heat exchange.
"Numerical Weather Prediction" (NWP) uses computers to solve complex fluid dynamics equations to forecast future weather.
"Ensemble Forecasting" runs a weather model many times with slight variations to see how "certain" a forecast is.
"Data Assimilation" is the process of feeding real-world observations (satellites, balloons, stations) into a computer model.
"Geostationary Satellites" stay over the same spot on Earth, providing continuous "movies" of cloud movement.
"Polar-Orbiting Satellites" orbit from pole to pole, providing high-resolution data for the entire globe twice a day.
"Doppler Radar" can "see" the wind inside a storm by measuring the shift in frequency of the reflected signal.
"Dual-Polarization Radar" allows meteorologists to distinguish between rain, snow, hail, and even biological targets like birds.
"Lidar" is similar to radar but uses laser light instead of radio waves, often used to measure wind and aerosols.
"Radiosondes" are small instrument packages carried by weather balloons to measure the vertical profile of the atmosphere.
"Dropsondes" are instruments dropped from airplanes to measure the conditions inside a hurricane as they fall.
"Anemometers" measure wind speed, while "Wind Vanes" measure wind direction.
"Hygrometers" measure humidity; the "Sling Psychrometer" is a classic version using two thermometers.
"Pyranometers" measure the amount of solar radiation reaching a surface.
"Ceilometers" use lasers to determine the exact height of the cloud base.
"ASOS" (Automated Surface Observing System) is the standard weather station used at airports around the world.
"Metars" are the standardized coded reports used by pilots to get the current weather at an airport.
"Isallobars" are lines on a map that connect points of equal pressure change over a specific time.
"Isotachs" are lines on a map connecting points of equal wind speed.
The "Hadley Cell" is the primary circulation loop where air rises at the equator and sinks at the subtropics (30° latitude).
The "Ferrel Cell" is the mid-latitude circulation where air moves poleward and eastward at the surface.
The "Polar Cell" is the circulation where cold air sinks at the poles and moves toward the mid-latitudes.
The "ITCZ" (Intertropical Convergence Zone) is the "weather equator" where the trade winds meet and cause constant rain.
"Trade Winds" are the steady easterly winds found in the tropics.
"Westerlies" are the prevailing winds in the mid-latitudes (like the US and Europe).
"Polar Easterlies" are the cold winds that blow away from the North and South Poles.
"Horse Latitudes" are the regions of calm winds and high pressure around 30° latitude.
"Doldrums" are the calm regions near the equator within the ITCZ.
A "Monsoon" is a seasonal shift in winds, usually bringing a dramatic change in precipitation (like the Indian Monsoon).
"Sea Breezes" are caused by the land heating up faster than the water during the day, drawing in cool air from the sea.
"Land Breezes" occur at night when the land cools faster than the water, blowing air from the land out to sea.
"Valley Breezes" occur as mountain slopes heat up in the sun, causing air to rise up the mountain.
"Mountain Breezes" occur at night as cold air sinks down the mountain slopes into the valleys.
"Chinook Winds" (or Foehn winds) are warm, dry winds that blow down the leeward side of mountains, melting snow rapidly.
"Santa Ana Winds" are hot, dry winds in California that blow from the desert toward the coast, often fueling wildfires.
"Katabatic Winds" are "gravity winds" where cold, dense air rushes down a slope (common in Antarctica and Greenland).
"Haboobs" are massive dust storms caused by the downdraft of a thunderstorm hitting a dry desert floor.
"Derechos" are long-lived, straight-line wind storms associated with fast-moving bands of severe thunderstorms.
"Squall Lines" are long chains of thunderstorms that often form ahead of a cold front.
"Supercells" are the most dangerous type of thunderstorm, characterized by a deep, rotating updraft called a mesocyclone.
"Wall Clouds" are localized lowerings of a supercell's base, often where a tornado will form.
"Shelf Clouds" are long, wedge-shaped clouds at the leading edge of a storm, indicating strong outflow.
"Mammatus Clouds" are pouch-like clouds that hang from the underside of a storm's anvil, indicating intense turbulence.
"Tornadoes" are violently rotating columns of air in contact with both the ground and a cumulonimbus cloud.
"Waterspouts" are simply tornadoes that form over water.
The "Enhanced Fujita Scale" (EF-Scale) rates tornadoes based on the damage they cause to structures and trees.
"Lightning" is a giant spark of static electricity caused by charge separation inside a cloud (usually ice vs. graupel).
"Thunder" is the sound of air expanding explosively as it is heated by a lightning bolt to 30,000°C.
A "Hurricane" is a tropical cyclone with sustained winds of at least 74 mph (119 km/h).
"Typhoons" are the exact same thing as hurricanes, just located in the Northwest Pacific.
"Cyclones" is the term used for these storms in the Indian Ocean and South Pacific.
The "Eye" of a hurricane is a region of calm winds and sinking air at the center of the storm.
The "Eyewall" is the ring of intense thunderstorms surrounding the eye, containing the storm's strongest winds.
"Storm Surge" is the rising of the sea level caused by a hurricane's wind and low pressure, responsible for most hurricane deaths.
"El Niño" is a climate pattern where the surface waters of the central and eastern Pacific become unusually warm.
"La Niña" is the opposite of El Niño, where Pacific waters become unusually cold.
The "Ozone Layer" in the stratosphere protects us from harmful UV radiation but is destroyed by chemicals like CFCs.
"Aerosol Indirect Effect" describes how pollution can make clouds more reflective, actually cooling the Earth slightly.
"Climate" is the long-term average of weather patterns, typically measured over 30 years or more.
"Paleoclimatology" is the study of ancient climates using ice cores, tree rings, and ocean sediments.
"Milankovitch Cycles" are long-term changes in Earth's orbit and tilt that trigger ice ages over thousands of years.
"Maunder Minimum" was a period of very low sunspot activity that coincided with the "Little Ice Age" in Europe.
"Global Warming" refers to the long-term rise in Earth's average temperature due to the accumulation of greenhouse gases.
"Ocean Acidification" is caused by the ocean absorbing excess CO2​, which lowers the pH of the water.
"Albedo Feedback" occurs as melting ice reflects less sunlight, causing more warming and more melting.
"Permafrost" is ground that stays frozen year-round; when it thaws, it releases methane, a potent greenhouse gas.
"Jet Streams" are narrow bands of very strong winds in the upper atmosphere that steer weather systems.
"Atmospheric Rivers" are long, narrow regions in the atmosphere that transport most of the water vapor outside of the tropics.
"Sky Glow" is the brightening of the night sky caused by artificial light reflecting off the atmosphere.
"Crepuscular Rays" are sunbeams that appear to radiate from a single point in the sky (usually the sun).
A "Halo" is a ring of light around the sun or moon caused by ice crystals in high cirrus clouds.
"Sundogs" are bright spots on either side of the sun, also caused by ice crystals.
A "Rainbow" is caused by reflection, refraction, and dispersion of light in water droplets.
The "Green Flash" is a rare optical phenomenon where a green spot is visible above the sun for a second at sunset.
"Fata Morgana" is a complex mirage that can make distant objects look like floating castles.
"Aurora Borealis" (Northern Lights) is caused by solar wind hitting the Earth's magnetic field and glowing in the upper atmosphere.
The "Inertial Circle" is the path an air parcel follows when the only forces acting on it are the Coriolis force and inertia, completing a full rotation in a "pendulum day."
"Rossby Radius of Deformation" in the atmosphere is roughly 1000 kilometers, defining the scale where rotation balances buoyancy.
"Baroclinic Instability" is the primary source of energy for mid-latitude storms, converting the potential energy of temperature gradients into kinetic energy.
"Vorticity Stretching" occurs when an air column is elongated vertically, causing its rotation to tighten and speed up, much like a figure skater pulling in their arms.
"Equivalent Potential Temperature" (θe​) is a "conserved" property, meaning it stays the same even if water condenses, making it perfect for tracking air mass origins.
"Isentropic Surfaces" are layers of constant potential temperature; air tends to move along these surfaces rather than crossing them.
"Potential Vorticity (PV) Anomalies" in the upper atmosphere can "induce" the formation of low-pressure systems at the surface.
The "Dynamic Tropopause" is often defined as the surface where the Potential Vorticity reaches a value of 1.5 to 2.0 PV units.
"Tropopause Folding" is a process where stratospheric air, rich in ozone and very dry, is pulled down into the troposphere near a jet streak.
"Ageostrophic Wind" is the "correction" to the geostrophic wind that actually allows for the vertical motion needed to create clouds and rain.
The "Q-G (Quasi-Geostrophic) Equations" are the simplified set of physics used by meteorologists to understand large-scale weather patterns without getting lost in small-scale noise.
"Differential Vorticity Advection" is a fancy way of saying that if rotation is increasing more quickly at higher altitudes, the air below must rise.
"Thickness" on a 1000–500 hPa chart is a direct proxy for the average temperature of the lower atmosphere; the "540 line" is often used as a rain-snow predictor.
"Jet Streaks" are localized pockets of even faster wind within a jet stream; their "left exit" and "right entrance" regions are the most likely spots for storm development.
"Secondary Circulation" refers to the vertical loops of air created by jet streaks that "vent" surface low-pressure systems.
"Frontogenesis" is the mathematical and physical process of a temperature gradient narrowing into a distinct weather front.
"Symmetric Instability" can create "slantwise convection," resulting in heavy bands of snow even when the atmosphere appears stable to traditional vertical tests.
The "Beta Effect" explains why large-scale planetary waves (Rossby waves) naturally move westward relative to the background wind.
"Zonal Flow" happens when the jet stream is straight and fast, keeping weather patterns moving quickly from west to east.
"Meridional Flow" happens when the jet stream becomes wavy, allowing Arctic air to plunge south and tropical air to surge north.
"Blocking" occurs when a high-pressure system becomes so strong it "breaks" the jet stream flow, leading to stagnant weather for weeks.
An "Omega Block" is named because the pressure contours look like the Greek letter Ω, trapping a warm high between two cold lows.
"Teleconnections" are statistical links between weather in one part of the world and another; for example, a warm Indian Ocean can change the winter in Canada.
The "PNA (Pacific-North American) Pattern" is a major teleconnection that determines whether the US East Coast faces a deep freeze or a warm spell.
"Arctic Amplification" describes how the Arctic is warming much faster than the rest of the planet, partly due to the loss of reflective sea ice.
"Stratospheric Sudden Warming" (SSW) is an event where the polar vortex breaks down, often leading to extreme cold snaps in the mid-latitudes weeks later.
"Hydrostatic Balance" is the assumption that the upward pressure of air perfectly balances the weight of the air above it; this is true for almost everything except thunderstorms.
"Convective Inhibition" (CIN) acts like a lid on a pot; it holds back energy until the surface heats up enough to "break the cap."
"Level of Free Convection" (LFC) is the point of no return for a rising air parcel; above this height, it is warmer than its surroundings and will accelerate upward.
"Equilibrium Level" (EL) is the height where a thunderstorm top begins to spread out, forming the classic "anvil" shape.
"Overshooting Tops" are dome-like bumps on top of an anvil cloud, signifying an updraft so powerful it punched into the stratosphere.
"Entrainment" is the "death" of many small clouds; as they rise, they pull in dry air from the sides, which causes their water droplets to evaporate.
"Cloud Condensation Nuclei" (CCN) are the seeds of clouds; without these microscopic bits of dust or salt, clouds would struggle to form even at 100% humidity.
"Supercooled Water" is liquid water that exists at temperatures as low as -40°C; it is extremely dangerous to aircraft because it freezes instantly on impact.
"Homogeneous Nucleation" is the process where water freezes without a "seed" particle, which only happens at temperatures below -38°C.
The "Bergeron Process" is why it rains in the mid-latitudes; ice crystals "steal" moisture from water droplets in a cloud until they are heavy enough to fall.
"Collision-Coalescence" is the primary rain-maker in the tropics; larger drops fall through a cloud and "gobble up" smaller drops in their path.
"Graupel" (snow pellets) forms when supercooled water "rimes" onto a snowflake, creating a tiny, soft hailstone.
"Hail" requires a "recirculation" process where an ice pellet is caught in an updraft multiple times, growing a new layer of ice each time it cycles.
"Terminal Velocity" of a large raindrop is about 9 meters per second; anything larger than 6mm usually breaks apart due to air resistance.
"Scattering" of sunlight by gas molecules is why the sky isn't black; "Rayleigh Scattering" favors blue, while "Mie Scattering" from clouds favors white.
"Albedo" of the Earth is about 0.3, meaning 30% of sunlight is bounced back into space without ever heating the planet.
"Greenhouse Gases" don't trap air like a real greenhouse; they absorb infrared "heat" radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back down to Earth.
"Water Vapor" is the strongest greenhouse gas on Earth, but its amount is controlled by temperature, not directly by human emissions.
"Radiative Forcing" is the measure of how much a specific gas (like CO2​) shifts the Earth's energy balance.
"Latent Heat of Vaporization" is 2.5 million Joules per kilogram; this is the massive amount of energy "hidden" in water vapor that fuels hurricanes.
"Sensible Heat" is the heat you can actually feel and measure with a thermometer.
A "Temperature Inversion" is the atmosphere's "security guard"; it prevents air from mixing vertically, which can trap smog in a city for days.
"Radiation Fog" is a "ground-up" fog that forms on clear nights as the Earth's surface radiates its heat away into space.
"Advection Fog" is "side-to-side" fog, formed when warm moist air slides over a cold surface (like a cold ocean current).
"Steam Fog" looks like a boiling lake; it happens when very cold air moves over warmer water.
"Lapse Rate" is the rate at which temperature drops with height; the "Dry" rate is 9.8°C/km, but the "Moist" rate is only about 6°C/km due to latent heat.
"Absolute Stability" occurs when the environmental lapse rate is very shallow; no matter how much you push an air parcel up, it will always be colder and sink back down.
"Conditional Instability" is the state of most "stormy" days; the air is stable if it's dry, but unstable if you can get it to start raining.
"Laminar Flow" is smooth and predictable, while "Turbulent Flow" is full of chaotic "eddies" that mix the air.
"Clear Air Turbulence" (CAT) is invisible and occurs when different layers of air move at different speeds (wind shear) without any clouds present.
"Mechanical Turbulence" is caused by the wind "tripping" over objects like trees, hills, or skyscrapers.
"Mountain Waves" are ripples in the air that can extend hundreds of miles downwind of a mountain range, much like waves behind a boat.
"Rotors" are dangerous, horizontal "drums" of rotating air that form under the crests of mountain waves.
A "Microburst" is a "wind bomb" that occurs when a thunderstorm downdraft hits the ground and spreads out at speeds up to 150 mph.
"St. Elmo's Fire" is not fire; it's a plasma discharge that glows blue around pointed objects during a strong electrical storm.
"Red Sprites" are massive, jellyfish-shaped electrical discharges that happen above a thunderstorm, 50 miles up in the mesosphere.
"Noctilucent Clouds" are made of ice crystals "seeded" by meteor dust at the very edge of space.
"Nacreous Clouds" are beautiful, iridescent clouds in the stratosphere that, unfortunately, provide the surface for chemical reactions that destroy ozone.
"Synoptic Scale" weather refers to features 1000km wide, like the high and low-pressure systems seen on a standard weather map.
"Mesoscale" weather refers to features like individual thunderstorms or sea breezes (1km to 100km).
"Numerical Weather Prediction" (NWP) works by dividing the atmosphere into millions of "cubes" and solving the laws of physics for each one.
"Ensemble Forecasting" involves running a model 50 different times with tiny changes to see if they all agree on the outcome.
"Chaos Theory" was discovered by a meteorologist (Edward Lorenz); he realized that even a tiny error in starting data makes a 2-week forecast nearly impossible.
"Doppler Radar" doesn't just see rain; it measures the "frequency shift" of the radio waves to tell which way the wind is blowing inside a storm.
"Dual-Pol Radar" uses vertical and horizontal pulses to tell the difference between a raindrop (flat) and a hailstone (round).
"Radiosondes" are launched twice daily worldwide at exactly 00:00 and 12:00 UTC to provide a 3D "snapshot" of the atmosphere.
"Dropsondes" are "reverse balloons" dropped from hurricane-hunter planes to measure a storm's pressure as they fall.
An "Anemometer" measures wind speed, but a "Sonic Anemometer" uses ultrasound to measure wind in 3D without any moving parts.
A "Hygrometer" measures humidity; the most accurate ones use a chilled mirror to find the exact "dew point" temperature.
"Pyranometers" measure "Global Solar Radiation," which includes both direct sunlight and the light scattered by the sky.
"Ceilometers" are laser-rangefinders that tell pilots exactly how high the bottom of the clouds are.
The "Hadley Cell" is why the tropics are rainy and the subtropics (30° latitude) are deserts; air rises at the equator and sinks at the "Horse Latitudes."
"Trade Winds" are the "return flow" of the Hadley cell, blowing from the northeast in the Northern Hemisphere.
The "ITCZ" (Intertropical Convergence Zone) is a "ring of storms" around the Earth where the trade winds from both hemispheres collide.
"Monsoons" are caused by a "seasonal sea breeze"; in summer, the land gets so hot it sucks in massive amounts of moist air from the ocean.
"Sea Breezes" occur because land has a lower "heat capacity" than water, meaning it heats up and cools down much faster.
"Chinook Winds" can raise the temperature by 50°F in minutes as air is compressed and heated while rushing down a mountain.
"Santa Ana Winds" gain their heat through "adiabatic compression" as they drop from the high deserts of Nevada down to the California coast.
"Katabatic Winds" are "gravity-powered"; cold air over a glacier becomes so heavy it "falls" down the slope like an invisible waterfall.
A "Derecho" is a "straight-line" wind storm that must travel at least 400 miles and produce winds of at least 58 mph to earn the name.
"Supercells" are unique because their updraft rotates; this rotation (mesocyclone) allows them to last for hours and produce tornadoes.
A "Wall Cloud" is a "lowered" cloud base that forms when a storm sucks in rain-cooled, moist air; it's the "pedestal" for a tornado.
"Shelf Clouds" are "outflow" clouds; they look scary but usually mean the storm is blowing "out" rather than sucking "in."
"Mammatus Clouds" are pouches of sinking air; they often appear on the "back side" of a severe storm as it moves away.
"Tornadoes" are measured on the "Enhanced Fujita Scale" based on the damage they do to "Indicator" objects like trees or houses.
"Lightning" is caused by "triboelectric charging"—the same thing as rubbing your socks on a carpet, but with ice crystals and graupel.
"Thunder" is the sound of a "sonic boom" as air expanded by lightning's heat collapses back on itself.
A "Hurricane" is a "warm-core" system; it's a giant stack of thunderstorms that creates a high-pressure "exhaust" at the very top.
"Storm Surge" is caused more by "wind push" than by the low pressure of the storm; it's a "bulge" of water that moves with the eye.
"El Niño" happens when the "Trade Winds" weaken, allowing warm water to slosh back toward South America.
The "Ozone Layer" is most depleted over Antarctica because "Polar Stratospheric Clouds" create the perfect surface for ozone-eating chemistry.
"Aerosol-Cloud Interactions" are the "wild card" of climate change; more pollution can actually make clouds "brighter" and more reflective.
"Climate" is what you expect (a snowy December), but "Weather" is what you get (a rainy Tuesday).
"Milankovitch Cycles" are the "metronome" of Earth's history, changing the planet's tilt and orbit over 100,000-year cycles.
"Maunder Minimum" was a 70-year period with almost no sunspots, which coincided with the Thames River in London freezing over.
"Ocean Acidification" happens because CO2​ reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid, making it harder for shellfish to build their shells.
"Albedo Feedback" is a "vicious cycle"; as snow melts, the dark ground absorbs more heat, which melts even more snow.
"Permafrost" stores twice as much carbon as the entire atmosphere; its thawing is one of the biggest "tipping points" in climate science.
"Jet Streams" are found where there is a sharp temperature "cliff" in the atmosphere; the bigger the temperature difference, the faster the jet.
"Atmospheric Rivers" can carry 15 times more water than the Mississippi River, but they do it in the form of invisible vapor.
"Sky Glow" is a form of light pollution that can disrupt the migratory patterns of birds that navigate by the stars.
"Crepuscular Rays" (Sunbeams) look like they spread out, but they are actually parallel; it's just a trick of perspective, like railroad tracks.
A "Halo" is a 22-degree ring around the sun caused by light passing through "hexagonal" ice crystals.
"Sundogs" (Parhelia) are "fake suns" that appear when those ice crystals are flat and oriented horizontally.
"Rainbows" are only visible when the sun is behind you and the rain is in front of you; the light reflects inside the drop.
"Green Flash" is a split-second event at sunset caused by the atmosphere acting like a prism and refracting green light last.
"Fata Morgana" mirages happen during a "temperature inversion"; light curves around the Earth's surface, showing you things that are over the horizon.
"Aurora" colors are determined by altitude: green is oxygen (lower), red is oxygen (higher), and purple/blue is nitrogen.
"Standard Gravity" is 9.80665m/s2, but it's actually weaker at the equator because the Earth's rotation "flings" you outward slightly.
"Geopotential Height" is a height coordinate that "warps" to stay consistent with the Earth's gravity field.
The "Gas Constant" (R) for dry air is different from the one for moist air, because water vapor is lighter than oxygen or nitrogen.
"Virtual Temperature" is the temperature dry air would need to be to have the same density as the current moist air.
"Hydrostatic Balance" is why the atmosphere doesn't just "fly off" into space or collapse into a thin sheet on the ground.
"Archimedes' Principle" in the air: a warm bubble of air rises because the "weight" of the air it displaces is greater than its own weight.
"Adiabatic" means "no heat in, no heat out"; as air rises, it does "work" to expand, and using that energy causes it to cool.
"Potential Temperature" (θ) is the temperature an air parcel would have if you "squashed" it down to sea level.
"Brunt-Väisälä Frequency" is the "heartbeat" of a stable atmosphere; if you push air up, it will "bounce" up and down at this specific rate.
"Ekman Spiral" in the ocean: wind pushes the top layer of water, which pushes the next layer, but each layer is deflected more by Coriolis.
"Reynolds Number" tells a meteorologist if a flow will be smooth or turbulent; the atmosphere almost always has a huge Reynolds number.
The "Navier-Stokes Equations" are the "Holy Grail" of weather prediction; they describe the motion of any fluid, but they are notoriously hard to solve.
"Numerical Diffusion" is an error where a computer model "blurs" a sharp weather front because the grid boxes are too big.
"Grid Spacing" of 3km is the "sweet spot" where a model can start to "see" individual thunderstorms without needing a supercomputer.
"Parameterization" is the "art" of telling a model how a cloud behaves using a simple math formula instead of calculating every drop.
"Data Assimilation" is like "calibrating" a model; it uses today's observations to fix the "guess" the model made yesterday.
The "Global Forecast System" (GFS) is the American "workhorse" model, providing free data to the entire world.
"Ensemble Mean" is the average of many model runs; it is usually more accurate than any single "member" of the ensemble.
"Teleconnection Indices" like the NAO or AO are the "short-hand" meteorologists use to describe the state of the global climate.
"Arctic Sea Ice" acts like a "mirror"; as it disappears, the "dark ocean" absorbs 90% of the sun's energy instead of reflecting it.
"Ocean Heat Content" is where 90% of the extra heat from global warming is actually stored.
"Thermohaline Circulation" is the "Great Ocean Conveyor Belt," driven by differences in "temperature" (thermo) and "salt" (haline).
"Carbon Sinks" like the Amazon rainforest are struggling to keep up with CO2​ emissions; some are even starting to release more than they absorb.
"Aerosols" like "Black Carbon" (soot) can melt glaciers faster by landing on the ice and absorbing sunlight.
"Geoengineering" is the controversial idea of "hacking" the weather by spraying sulfuric acid into the stratosphere to mimic a volcano.
"Urban Heat Islands" can be 10°F warmer than the surrounding countryside because bricks and asphalt "soak up" sunlight all day.
"Biometeorology" explores how the "Falling Barometer" can trigger migraines or joint pain in sensitive people.
"Phenology" shows that spring is arriving earlier; flowers are blooming and birds are nesting weeks before they did 50 years ago.
"Growing Degree Days" is a "math-based" way for farmers to know when to harvest, based on how much "heat" the crop has experienced.
"Aviation Weather" warnings for "Icing" are most common in "Stratiform" clouds where supercooled water is widespread.
"Carburetor Icing" can crash a small plane even on a 70°F day if the humidity is high enough to freeze inside the engine's intake.
"TKE (Turbulent Kinetic Energy)" is the "vigor" of the air's churn; pilots want this number to be as low as possible.
"Mountain Rotors" have been known to flip small aircraft upside down; they are the most dangerous form of "hidden" turbulence.
"Downsloping Winds" (Chinooks) are "dry" because the air lost all its moisture while being pushed up the other side of the mountain.
"Mistral" winds in France are so strong and persistent they have actually influenced the way people build their houses.
"Bora" winds in the Adriatic can be so cold and fast they coat ships in "ice-armor" within minutes.
"Sirocco" winds from Africa are the reason you can sometimes find "red dust" from the Sahara on cars in London or Paris.
"Harmattan" winds in West Africa can create a "haze" so thick that airports have to close due to low visibility.
"Willy-Willy" is a playful Australian name for a "Dust Devil," which is essentially a "mini-tornado" powered by surface heat.
"Thunderstorm Asthma" is a real medical emergency; storms break pollen into tiny pieces that can get deep into the lungs.
"Red Tides" can be "blown" toward the shore by certain wind patterns, making the air at the beach hard to breathe.
"Sandstorms" are "gravity currents"; the heavy, dust-filled air acts like a liquid, "flowing" across the desert floor.
"Ice Storms" are the "stealthiest" weather; they look like rain, but they turn trees into "glass sculptures" that eventually snap under the weight.
"Thundersnow" requires a "perfect storm" of cold air and intense upward "lift," often found near the center of a "Bomb Cyclone."
"Sun Pillars" are vertical "beams" of light caused by "plate-like" ice crystals reflecting sunlight like a million tiny mirrors.
"Glories" are seen from airplanes or mountaintops; they are "rainbow-halos" that surround your own shadow on a cloud.
"Heiligenschein" is a German word for the "holy glow" you see around your shadow's head when looking at dewy grass.
"Anti-Solar Point" is the "bullseye" of every rainbow; if you could see the whole thing, it would be a perfect circle centered on this point.
"Refractive Index" is why stars twinkle (scintillation); as their light passes through different layers of air, it "wiggles" on its way to your eye.
